OUR MISSION

The greatest recession of 2007-2009 was called a “man-cession” because the hardest hit sectors were traditionally male: construction and manufacturing. However, in 2020, the word was newly branded “she-cession” as the worst hit industries are those that can’t be done virtually and are largely dominated by women such as travel, education, health services and retail. Now more than ever, it is vital for women to have financial security, therefore, this is what led us to create a website that provides women with resources on how to utilise money and to provide women with financial literacy. It is our mission to educate women on finance particularly during uncertain times where women have accounted for 55% of job losses during this pandemic. Investing in women’s financial literacy is the key to achieving financial and social equality. As more women become leaders across the globe, it’s imperative to ensure that they have access to a robust and impactful financial education. With greater economic independence and empowerment, there are no limits to what we as women can achieve.

Welcome to Her Finance, created by women, for women.

Paying off Debt

Women are more likely than men to struggle with debt, with women making up 55% of over-indebted people in the UK, and being 14% more likely than men to go insolvent, according to research by Money Advice Service. The reasons why women are more likely to accumulate debt include the fact that women are paid less than men (15.5% less in 2020), more likely to be in lower-paid jobs and more likely to leave work or go part- time to take care of caring responsibilities. Saving

Research reveals a dramatic difference in the amount of money British men and women have in their savings, exposing a 32% gender savings gap. The consumer study, by leading discount site VoucherCodes.co.uk, reveals that the average man in Britain has £24,880 in savings – 32% more (£8,038) than the average British woman. The widest gender savings gap is found among millennials, with female respondents in that age group reporting 60% less in savings than their male counterparts – an enormous £15.9k. Investing

Only 13% of women think they have a good amount of knowledge and understanding of investing and stock markets. A study, led by YouGov Omnibus, found that over half (52%) of UK women have never owned any investments, compared to 37% of men. The study also reveals that only one in five women hold an investment, against a third of men.
